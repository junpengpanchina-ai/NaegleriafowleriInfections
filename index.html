KEY POINTS

  • Naegleria fowleri is a free-living amoeba, a kind of one-celled organism that thrives in warm freshwater lakes, rivers, and hot springs.
  • It is often called the "brain-eating amoeba" because it can infect the brain and destroy brain tissue.
  • Brain infections caused by Naegleria fowleri are very rare but nearly always fatal.

MORE INFORMATION

Initial symptoms of PAM (Primary Amebic Meningoencephalitis) start about 5 days after infection and may include:

  • Headache
  • Fever
  • Nausea or vomiting
  • Stiff neck

Later symptoms may include seizures, altered mental status, hallucinations, and focal neurologic deficits.

You can reduce your risk by following these prevention tips:

  • Avoid jumping or diving into warm freshwater
  • Hold your nose shut or use nose clips when jumping or diving
  • Avoid putting your head underwater in hot springs and thermally-polluted water
  • Avoid water activities in warm freshwater during periods of high water temperature

Treatment for PAM is difficult because the infection progresses rapidly. Several drugs have shown promise in laboratory studies, including:

  • Amphotericin B
  • Azithromycin
  • Fluconazole
  • Rifampin
  • Miltefosine

Early diagnosis and treatment are critical. If you have symptoms after recent freshwater activities, seek medical attention immediately.

ABOUT NAEGLERIA FOWLERI

Naegleria fowleri is a free-living amoeba (a single-celled living organism). It causes a disease called primary amebic meningoencephalitis (PAM), a brain infection that leads to the destruction of brain tissue.

The amoeba is commonly found in warm freshwater environments such as lakes, rivers, and hot springs. It can also be found in soil, swimming pools, and water heaters that are not properly maintained.

CAUSES & TRANSMISSION

Infection occurs when contaminated water enters the body through the nose. The amoeba then travels to the brain where it causes PAM, which is usually fatal.

Important: You cannot get infected by drinking contaminated water. The amoeba must enter through the nasal cavity.

CLINICAL FEATURES

Healthcare providers should be aware of the clinical presentation of PAM:

  • Stage 1 (1-2 days): Fever, headache, nausea, vomiting
  • Stage 2 (3-5 days): Stiff neck, confusion, hallucinations, seizures
  • Stage 3 (5+ days): Loss of attention, poor coordination, coma

Symptoms typically appear 1-9 days after infection, with death occurring 1-18 days after symptom onset.

CLINICAL TESTING & DIAGNOSIS

Early and accurate diagnosis is critical for patient management:

  • Lumbar puncture: CSF shows elevated white blood cells, protein, and low glucose
  • Microscopy: Direct visualization of motile amoebic trophozoites in fresh CSF
  • PCR testing: Real-time PCR available at CDC and some state labs
  • Antigen detection: EIA testing available

Contact CDC immediately if PAM is suspected: 770-488-7100

PUBLIC HEALTH GUIDANCE

Public health officials should implement comprehensive prevention and response strategies:

  • Surveillance systems for PAM cases
  • Environmental assessment protocols
  • Public communication strategies
  • Healthcare provider education
  • Water system monitoring guidelines

ACANTHAMOEBA INFECTIONS

Acanthamoeba species are free-living amoebae that can cause serious infections in humans, including keratitis (corneal infection) and granulomatous amebic encephalitis (GAE).

Key Differences from Naegleria fowleri:

  • Route of infection: Acanthamoeba typically infects through the eye (keratitis) or skin wounds
  • Disease progression: Generally more chronic than PAM caused by Naegleria fowleri
  • Treatment: May respond to combination antimicrobial therapy
  • Risk factors: Contact lens use, corneal trauma, immunocompromise

RECREATIONAL WATER SAFETY

Recreational water activities can expose individuals to various waterborne pathogens. Understanding water safety is crucial for preventing infections.

Water Safety Guidelines:

  • Swimming Pools: Ensure proper chlorination and pH levels
  • Natural Water Bodies: Be aware of water temperature and quality advisories
  • Hot Tubs/Spas: Regular maintenance and appropriate disinfection
  • Water Activities: Avoid activities that force water up the nose

Common Recreational Water Illnesses (RWIs):

Bacterial

E. coli, Shigella, Legionella

Viral

Norovirus, Adenovirus

Parasitic

Cryptosporidium, Giardia, Naegleria

Chemical

Chlorine exposure, pH imbalance

PARASITIC DISEASES

Parasitic diseases affect millions of people worldwide. Understanding the different types of parasites and their transmission helps in prevention and treatment.

Types of Parasites:

Protozoa (Single-celled)
  • Free-living amoebae: Naegleria, Acanthamoeba, Balamuthia
  • Intestinal parasites: Giardia, Cryptosporidium, Entamoeba
  • Blood parasites: Malaria, Babesia, Trypanosoma
Helminths (Worms)
  • Roundworms: Ascaris, Hookworm, Pinworm
  • Flatworms: Tapeworms, Flukes
  • Tissue parasites: Trichinella, Filarial worms
Ectoparasites
  • Arthropods: Lice, Fleas, Ticks
  • Mites: Scabies, Chiggers
  • Other: Bedbugs, Mosquitoes

Prevention Strategies:

  • Safe water and food practices
  • Personal hygiene measures
  • Vector control
  • Travel health precautions
  • Regular medical screening in endemic areas
